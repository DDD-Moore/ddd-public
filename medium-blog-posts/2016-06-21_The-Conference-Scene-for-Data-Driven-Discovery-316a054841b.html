The Conference Scene for Data-Driven Discovery

By Carly Strasser

Do you ever get FOMO when you see a conference hashtag on Twitter? We do. Luckily, we have a way to find out what conferences are most important to our grantees in Data-Driven Discovery at the Moore Foundation: annual reports. As part of our yearly fact-gathering from grantees, we request information about the conferences they have attended in the last year. We collected this information from our 14 DDD Investigators and more than 100 researchers from the Moore-Sloan Data Science Environments, with representatives from many different disciplines, career stages, and methodological approaches. Below is a list of the 30 most attended conferences last year, in alphabetical order:

  • American Chemical Society Special Interest Group on Management Of Data (ACM SIGMOD)
  • American Astronomical Society (AAS) Annual Meeting
  • American Geophysical Union (AGU) Annual Meeting
  • American Physical Society (APS) Meetings
  • American Political Science Association (APSA) Annual Conference
  • Association of Internet Researchers (AoIR)
  • Astro Hack Week
  • Bloomberg Data4Good Exchange (D4GX)
  • Ecological Society of America (ESA) Annual Meeting
  • Eurovis
  • IEEE VIS
  • International Conference on Machine Learning
  • ISCB: Intelligent Systems for Molecular Biology
  • Joint Statistical Meetings
  • JuliaCon
  • Mozilla MozFest
  • Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS)
  • Pacific Symposium on Biocomputing
  • PyCon

  • RECOMB Regulatory Genomics
  • SciPy

  • SIGKDD: Asociation for Computing Machinery’s Special Interest Group on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining
  • Society for Integrative and Comparative Biology (SICB)
  • Society for the Social Studies of Science
  • Supercomputing
  • UseR

  • Working towards Sustainable Software for Science: Practice and Experiences (WSSSPE)

A digression: “Why do you require those pesky annual reports?”

Annual reports are the yearly documents we require from those we fund to better understand their work, as well as its reach and impact. Anyone who’s ever had to submit a report to a funder knows this task can be tedious, but the data and information we collect is vital to helping others (especially our boards, in the case of private foundations) to understand the importance of what our grantees are accomplishing. If all goes well, maybe we can get more money to fund people and projects like you!
